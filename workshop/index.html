The playbook is the infrastructure
that ensures signal arrives.

The dodecahedron — twelve pentagonal faces, each identical, each a different angle on the same whole — is the geometry associated with the cosmos and with signal broadcast through space. Transmission is the step where the direction chosen in Step 5 gets encoded into a living document: the financial ops playbook. Not a policy manual. Not a set of rules. A shared language for how financial information and decisions move through the team — so the signal doesn't have to travel through one person every time.

"The path: birds eat the seed before it lands. The playbook is the infrastructure that ensures signal travels and arrives."

A well-built playbook doesn't centralise decisions — it distributes the intelligence required to make them. Every function gets a voice. Every conversation has a structure. The CEO stops being the single point through which all financial signal must pass.

The playbook — four sections

Section 1

Decision rights

Who decides what, at what threshold, without needing to ask. What requires the full team. What one person can move on alone.

Section 2

Communication cadence

How often the financial picture is shared, in what format, with whom. The rhythm that keeps everyone calibrated without every meeting becoming a finance meeting.

Section 3

Rules of engagement

How disagreements about financial decisions get resolved. Who gets a vote. Who makes the final call. What happens when the signal is contested.

Section 4

Living document protocol

How the playbook updates as the system changes. Who proposes revisions. How often it's reviewed. What triggers a full revisit.

Why the playbook comes before governance

Step 7 will distribute decision-making across the team. But distributed decision-making without a shared language is chaos — not coherence. The playbook is the prerequisite for decentralisation. It encodes the intelligence — the values, the vector, the rules of engagement — so that decisions can be made without the founder present. The playbook is what allows the Egg of Life (Step 7) to function: seven complete nodes, each able to express itself, because each knows the shared language.

The rules of engagement — why they matter most

Of the four sections, the rules of engagement tend to be the most generative and the most avoided. Most teams have implicit rules — everyone knows that certain conversations don't happen in certain rooms, that certain people's opinions carry more weight than they should, that certain decisions get made and then relitigated. The rules of engagement make the implicit explicit. Named rules can be agreed on, revised, and held. Unnamed rules just operate — often inequitably, always invisibly.

Transmission signals — what to listen for

Signal lands

Financial information is shared proactively, not extracted reactively. Team members can describe the financial picture in their own words. Decisions get made and stay made — they don't get relitigated in the next meeting.

Signal gets eaten

Financial information lives in one person's head or one person's spreadsheet. The rest of the team operates on assumptions. Decisions made in one conversation are unmade in another. The same discussion keeps happening.

Signal is blocked

There are financial conversations the team avoids — pricing, compensation, runway — because there's no shared language or agreed process for having them. The silence around these topics is itself a signal.

The playbook as a living document

The most common mistake with playbooks is treating them as finished artifacts. A playbook that doesn't update is a policy document — and policy documents calcify. The living document protocol in Section 4 is what keeps the playbook coherent over time. It answers: who can propose a change, how changes get ratified, and what triggers a full revisit rather than a minor update. The playbook should be as alive as the system it describes.

What this step tends to produce

The most common revelation in Step 6 is that the financial conversations the team has been avoiding are avoided precisely because there's no agreed structure for having them. The rules of engagement exercise tends to surface this directly — when the team is asked to name how disagreements get resolved, they often realise there is no process, only whoever is most persistent or most senior. Naming this is not an indictment. It is the beginning of transmission: a shared language where the signal can finally travel.

This exercise builds the first draft of your financial ops playbook. It is a prototype — not a finished document. Write quickly and honestly. A rough playbook that reflects the actual system is more useful than a polished one that describes the system you wish you had. The living document protocol in Section 4 is how it gets refined from here.

The Sower question at step 6

The playbook is not the destination. It is the infrastructure. A team in good soil will use it and update it. A team in thorny ground will complete this exercise and file it. The test of the playbook is not whether it's written — it's whether it's used the next time a contested financial decision arises.

Step 7 takes what's been built here and distributes it. The Egg of Life cannot function without the shared language the playbook encodes. Every node needs to be able to express itself fully — and that requires knowing the rules of the field it's expressing into.
